THE KOITOR HERITAGE[cite: 1]

Origins and Identity of the Gond Tribe[cite: 1]

Comprehensive Exam Study Notes[cite: 1]

1. OVERVIEW & NATIONAL SIGNIFICANCE[cite: 1]

1.1 Global & National Status[cite: 1]

  • Global: One of the largest tribal groups in the world.[cite: 1]
  • National: The LARGEST tribe in India.[cite: 1]

1.2 State Level — Madhya Pradesh (MP)[cite: 1]

  • Rank: 2nd largest tribe in MP (after the Bhil tribe).[cite: 1]
  • Top 3 tribes in Madhya Pradesh: (1) Bhil, (2) Gond, (3) Kol[cite: 1]

2. ORIGIN / IDENTITY[cite: 1]

2.1 Species Group[cite: 1]

  • Racial Category: Proto-Australoid[cite: 1]

2.2 Linguistic Family[cite: 1]

  • Language Family: Dravidian Family[cite: 1]

2.3 Expert View[cite: 1]

  • Anthropologist Christoph von Fürer-Haimendorf: States that Dravidians likely arrived by sea route concurrently with Aryans.[cite: 1]

3. ETYMOLOGY — THE WORD 'GOND'[cite: 1]

3.1 Origin of the Name[cite: 1]

  • Source Word: 'Konda' (Telugu language)[cite: 1]
  • Meaning: Mountain (Parvat)[cite: 1]
  • Habitat Connection: They inhabited the Vindhya & Satpura mountain ranges, hence the name.[cite: 1]
  • Result: 'Konda' evolved into 'Gond'[cite: 1]

3.2 Alternative Theory (British Era)[cite: 1]

  • General Cunningham accepted the derivation from the Bengali language.[cite: 1]

4. KEY SCHOLARS & HISTORICAL LITERATURE[cite: 1]

4.1 Stephen Hislop — The Researcher[cite: 1]

  • Role: Pioneer researcher on Gond origins.[cite: 1]
  • Key Work: Aboriginal Tribes of the Central Provinces[cite: 1]
  • Notable Contribution: Mentioned the concept of 'Lingo Mithkal' (a concept associated with the tribe).[cite: 1]

4.2 Sir Richard Temple — The Publisher[cite: 1]

  • Role: Posthumous Publisher of Hislop's work.[cite: 1]
  • Published: Hislop's critical study in the year 1866.[cite: 1]

5. DEMOGRAPHICS — CENSUS 2011[cite: 1]

5.1 Key Figures[cite: 1]

  • Total Gond Population in Madhya Pradesh: 50.93 Lakh[cite: 1]
  • Share of MP's Total Tribal Population: 33.25%[cite: 1]

5.2 Highest Concentration District[cite: 1]

  • District: Chhindwara (Chhindwara — the district with the highest Gond population)[cite: 1]
  • Population in Chhindwara: 6.2 Lakh[cite: 1]
  • Literacy Rate in Chhindwara: 60.1%[cite: 1]

6. GEOGRAPHICAL DISTRIBUTION[cite: 1]

6.1 Core Region[cite: 1]

  • Main Region: South-Eastern Madhya Pradesh (Dakshin-Purv MP)[cite: 1]
  • Spread: Across both sides of the Narmada Valley[cite: 1]
  • Notable Absence: Generally absent in the Northern part of MP[cite: 1]

6.2 Major Districts[cite: 1]

The Gond tribe is present across the following major districts of MP:[cite: 1]

Mandla, Dindori, Balaghat, Seoni, Chhindwara, Betul, Narmadapuram, Pandhurna, Shahdol, Sagar, Damoh, Satna, Khandwa, Burhanpur, Sehore, Raisen, Sevon, Narsinghpur[cite: 1]

7. PHYSICAL CHARACTERISTICS & TRAITS[cite: 1]

7.1 Origin Classification[cite: 1]

  • Origin: Dravidian Origin (Dravid Mool)[cite: 1]

7.2 Physical Features[cite: 1]

  • Straight hair[cite: 1]
  • Dark-black complexion[cite: 1]
  • Wide mouths and large, flat noses[cite: 1]
  • Thick lips[cite: 1]
  • Short stature — women are typically shorter than men[cite: 1]

7.3 Personality Traits[cite: 1]

  • Polite, truthful, and trustworthy[cite: 1]

8. CLASSIFICATION OF GOND TRIBE[cite: 1]

8.1 Occupational Sub-Tribes (IMPORTANT)[cite: 1]

The Gond tribe is classified into several sub-tribes based on their traditional occupation:[cite: 1]

Sub-Tribe Occupation
Agariya (Agariya)[cite: 1] Iron workers — work with iron/metal[cite: 1]
Pardhan (Pradhan)[cite: 1] Priests who perform puja in temples[cite: 1]
Ojha (Ojha)[cite: 1] Engaged in scholarly & tantric activities[cite: 1]
Koilabhuti (Koilabhuti)[cite: 1] Dancers & singers[cite: 1]
Solhas (Solhas)[cite: 1] Carpenters (Badhai)[cite: 1]

8.2 Status-Based Administrative Classification[cite: 1]

  • Raj Gond (Raja Gond): Elite class and landowners — highest social status[cite: 1]
  • Dhur / Dbool Gond (Dhur / Dhol Gond): Ordinary / general class[cite: 1]
  • Muria / Maria Gond (Muria / Maria Gond): Extremely backward caste — lowest social status[cite: 1]

8.3 Additional Classifications[cite: 1]

  • MP Government Records: Recognizes 50+ sub-branches including Asur, Badi Maria, Bhuta, Bison-horn Maria, Dorla, Gowari, etc.[cite: 1]
  • Scholar Stephen Fuchs: Notes 4 distinct social classes in Mandla district — e.g., Dev Gond, Suryavanshi Gond.[cite: 1]
  • Other noted sub-types: Suryavanshi, Devgadi, Ravan vanshi.[cite: 1]

9. GOTRA / CLAN SYSTEM (KUR SYSTEM)[cite: 1]

9.1 Local Terminology[cite: 1]

  • Local term for 'Gotra': 'Kur' (Kur)[cite: 1]
  • Note: In Bhil tribe, 'Ku' means house (for comparison).[cite: 1]

9.2 Classification of Gotras[cite: 1]

  • Based on Worship of Gods: Clans are named after the 7 homes of gods.[cite: 1]
  • Based on Devguri (Devgudi): This method yields 62 Gotras.[cite: 1]

9.3 Seven Homes of Gods[cite: 1]

  • Parati / Hansiya[cite: 1]
  • Pass / Bakhar[cite: 1]
  • Kulhadi[cite: 1]
  • Basula[cite: 1]
  • Kusia[cite: 1]
  • Khusi[cite: 1]
  • 7th God: Ropi (Ropi) — resides in the house itself.[cite: 1]

10. FAMILY STRUCTURE & DYNAMICS[cite: 1]

10.1 Patriarchal System[cite: 1]

  • Head: The oldest male member (Grandfather / Dada) is the absolute head.[cite: 1]
  • Strict obedience is demanded from all family members.[cite: 1]

10.2 Matriarchal Influence[cite: 1]

  • Grandmother (Dadi): Holds the 2nd place of importance in the household.[cite: 1]
  • She is revered as the mistress of the house.[cite: 1]

10.3 Cultural Trait — Hospitality[cite: 1]

  • Hospitality (Atithi Satkar) is paramount.[cite: 1]
  • Every house maintains a clean, dedicated small room specifically for guests.[cite: 1]

12. VILLAGE PANCHAYAT HIERARCHY[cite: 1]

12.1 Hierarchy Structure[cite: 1]

The Gond village panchayat follows a strictly defined hierarchy:[cite: 1]

  • 1st — Bhoi (Bhoi): Head of the village; also called Manjhi or Deshmukh (setteled internal maters).[cite: 1]
  • 2nd — Muqaddam (Muqaddam): Second in command.[cite: 1]
  • 3rd — Dewan (Diwan): Third position.[cite: 1]
  • 4th — Likhai (Likhai): Elderly members of the village.[cite: 1]

12.2 Key Officials & Their Roles[cite: 1]

  • Muqaddam / Gotiya: Village head. Traditional/hereditary role (passed from father to son). Responsible for collecting land revenue.[cite: 1]
  • Kotwar (Kotwar): Assistant to Muqaddam. Acts as liaison for visiting officials. Sole record-keeper of deaths and thefts for the police.[cite: 1]
  • Dewan (Diwan): Provides secret, case-related intelligence to the chief.[cite: 1]
  • Guniya / Barua / Panda: The spiritual authority. Performs Tantric rituals in the village.[cite: 1]

13. VILLAGE & RESIDENCE PATTERNS[cite: 1]

13.1 Historical Habitat[cite: 1]

  • Found in inaccessible hilly areas and dense forests.[cite: 1]

13.2 Modern Settlement[cite: 1]

  • Shifted to plain areas based on: arable land, water availability, and security.[cite: 1]

13.3 Traditional Housing[cite: 1]

  • Houses are typically made of mud and thatch/trash (mitti aur chhappar).[cite: 1]

14. FOOD & DIET[cite: 1]

14.1 General Diet[cite: 1]

  • Type: Both Vegetarian and Non-Vegetarian.[cite: 1]
  • Staple Diet: Includes Millets and Roots (Bajra and Kand-Mool).[cite: 1]

14.2 Key Food/Drink Terms[cite: 1]

  • Pej (Pej): A highly favored traditional drink made from coarse grains.[cite: 1]
  • Devanna (Devanna): The local tribal term for Mahua (a plant/flower used in food and liquor).[cite: 1]

15. TRADITIONAL ATTIRE & ADORNMENT[cite: 1]

15.1 Men's Attire[cite: 1]

  • Wear head turbans.[cite: 1]
  • Mostly wear clothing below the waist — cotton clothes and loincloths.[cite: 1]
  • Modern clothing is increasingly being adopted.[cite: 1]

15.2 Women's Adornment[cite: 1]

  • High prevalence of tattooing — known as 'Godna'.[cite: 1]
  • Ornaments made of brass, pearl, and coral are highly popular.[cite: 1]

16. BIRTH RITUALS & TRADITIONS[cite: 1]

16.1 Timeline of Birth Rituals[cite: 1]

  • Day 0 — Birth: Takes place at home under a makeshift cloth cover. The midwife cuts the umbilical cord with a bamboo strip and buries it on the spot.[cite: 1]
  • Day 6: Sighting Ritual — The baby is traditionally shown the Pole Star (Dhruv Tara).[cite: 1]
  • Day 12: Naming Ceremony (Namkaran) — The baby is officially named.[cite: 1]

17. SOCIETY & MARRIAGE RULES[cite: 1]

17.1 Allowed Practices[cite: 1]

  • Widow Remarriage: Allowed.[cite: 1]
  • Divorce: Allowed.[cite: 1]
  • Exogamy: Marriage outside one's clan (Gotra) is required — Bahir-vivah.[cite: 1]
  • General Rule: Monogamy(single marriage) is standard. Polygamy (bahu vivah) is practiced by rich/prosperous Gonds.[cite: 1]

17.2 Prohibited Practices[cite: 1]

  • Endogamous marriage: Prohibited — marriage within the exact same clan is strictly forbidden (Sagotriya Vivah).[cite: 1]

18. UNIQUE & SPECIAL MARRIAGE TYPES (MCQ FOCUS)[cite: 1]

18.1 Doodh Lautawa (Dudh Lautawa) — Milk Return Practice[cite: 1]

  • Type: Famous cross-cousin marriage.[cite: 1]
  • Example: Marriage between a brother's son and a sister's daughter.[cite: 1]
  • This is a very famous and widely practiced custom among the Gonds.[cite: 1]

18.2 Seva Marriage / Lamsena (Seva Vivah)[cite: 1]

  • Also called: Lamsena Marriage[cite: 1]
  • Description: Service Marriage — if the groom is unable to pay the bride price (Doodh Bandh / Dudh Bandh), he serves at the father-in-law's house instead.[cite: 1]
  • Special Term: The groom in this marriage is called Lamanai (Lamanai).[cite: 1]

18.3 Chad Marriage (Chad Vivah)[cite: 1]

  • Direction: Groom's procession goes to the bride's house.[cite: 1]
  • Special Note: Widow women take circumambulations (phere) with a water creature during this ritual.[cite: 1]

18.4 Pathoni Marriage (Pathoni Vivah)[cite: 1]

  • Direction: Reverse procession — the girl's wedding procession goes to the boy's house.[cite: 1]

18.5 Hatth / Bhageli (Hath Vivah / Bhageli)[cite: 1]

  • Description: Marriage by consent — the girl runs away to the boy's house (Sehmat se vivah).[cite: 1]

18.6 Polyether / Payasotur (Payasotur)[cite: 1]

  • Description: Represents a form of abduction marriage (apharan karke)within the tribe.[cite: 1]

18.7 Forced Marriage (Jabran Vivah)[cite: 1]

  • Occurrence: Occurs when there is parental disagreement.[cite: 1]
  • Boy brings the girl forcibly OR sprinkles turmeric (haldi) on her in the market.[cite: 1]
  • The Mandap is set up at night with music.[cite: 1]

18.8 Priti Marriage (Priti Vivah)[cite: 1]

  • Key Feature: Compulsory bride price (Vadhu Mulya / Doodh Bandh) must be given to the girl's father.[cite: 1]

18.9 Family Rights[cite: 1]

  • There is a moral right to marry into both paternal and maternal families.[cite: 1]

20. MANDAP TERMINOLOGY (WEDDING PAVILION TERMS)[cite: 1]

  • Mangar Mati (Manger Mati): Setting up the Mandap — the ritual practice of establishing the wedding pavilion.[cite: 1]
  • Mandwa-Tarapauni (Mandwa-Tarapauni): Liquor given to the Mandap builders as a gift/offering.[cite: 1]
  • Mandwa Tari (Mandwa Tari): Refers to three specific types of Mandap marriages.[cite: 1]

21. GHOTUL (YOUTH DORMITORY SYSTEM)[cite: 1]

21.1 Key Facts[cite: 1]

  • Origin: Started by Deity Lingo (Lingo Devta).[cite: 1]
  • Concept: Youth Dormitory — a social institution for unmarried boys and girls.[cite: 1]
  • Purpose: Pre-marriage sex education (Vivah Purv Yaun Shiksha).[cite: 1]
  • Social Function: Freedom to form romantic relationships (Prem Sambandh banane ki Swatantrata).[cite: 1]

22. TRIBAL DANCES[cite: 1]

22.1 Karma Dance (Karma Nritya) — VERY IMPORTANT[cite: 1]

  • Significance: World's FIRST folk dance to be included in the Guinness Book of World Records.[cite: 1]
  • Gender: Female-dominated dance.[cite: 1]
  • Primary Instrument: Mandar (Mandar drum).[cite: 1]
  • Classification: Main/primary dance of the Gond tribe.[cite: 1]

22.2 Bhadauni Dance (Bhadauni)[cite: 1]

  • Type: Marriage Dance (Vivah Nritya).[cite: 1]
  • Women playfully tease/abuse the groom's side with sweet songs sung under the Mandap.[cite: 1]

22.3 Saila Dance (Saila Nritya)[cite: 1]

  • Gender: Men only — exclusively performed by males.[cite: 1]
  • Location: Performed on rocks or mountains.[cite: 1]
  • Name Origin: Derived from 'Saila' meaning 'Rock' (Chattan).[cite: 1]

23. MAJOR TRIBAL ART FORMS[cite: 1]

23.1 Digna Art (Digna Kala)[cite: 1]

  • Creator: Women — created on walls and floors.[cite: 1]
  • Medium: Cow dung and soil (Gobar aur mitti).[cite: 1]
  • Occasions: Marriages, Diwali, Hariyali Amavasya, Holi, and Fairs.[cite: 1]

23.2 Nohadora Art (Nohadora Kala)[cite: 1]

  • Style: Raised lines of clay (Mitti ki ubhari hui rekhayen).[cite: 1]
  • Subject: Flora and Fauna — depicted on wet walls.[cite: 1]
  • Occasion: When building a new house.[cite: 1]

24. DEATH RITUALS & CUSTOMS[cite: 1]

24.1 Supreme Deity — Bada Dev[cite: 1]

  • Bada Dev (Bada Dev): The sole creator and raiser of man (Manushya ko janm dene aur paalne wala ekmatra dev).[cite: 1]

24.2 Disposal of the Dead[cite: 1]

  • Natural Death: Cremation (Dah Sanskar).[cite: 1]
  • Death Caused by Disease: Burial (Dafnana).[cite: 1]

24.3 The 10th Day — Dashmani Rituals (IMPORTANT)[cite: 1]

  • Dashmani / Dashagatra: The main 10-day funeral rite.[cite: 1]
  • Pato-Pratha (Pato-Pratha): The widow wears bangles in front of the Panchas (village elders).[cite: 1]
  • Mordan (Mordan): Death donation — items such as clothes and goods given at the time of death.[cite: 1]
  • Negi / Dasaundhi: The receiver of the Mordan donation.[cite: 1]

24.4 Ancestor Worship & Sacred Songs[cite: 1]

  • Khaul Pata (Khaul Pata): A specific death song sung during funeral rites.[cite: 1]
  • Nawakhani (Nawakhani): A festival dedicated to the worship of Gond ancestors.[cite: 1]
